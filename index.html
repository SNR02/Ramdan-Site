HOW DO
MUSLIMS OBSERVE?




Fasting during Ramadan is among the greatest acts of worship for Muslims. It cleanses the mind, body, and soul from the spiritual and physical impurities of this world, and brings Muslims from around the world together as they endeavor to practice the virtue of self -discipline together. There is a vibrant community atmosphere and an increased number of family gatherings.

Fasting is an act of worship that deprives the body to satiate the hungry soul.

FASTING




During Ramadan, Muslims refrain from eating, drinking, smoking and intimacy from dawn to dusk, while increasing their focus on prayer, family, and charity.

Fasting is obligatory for all adult Muslims, except those who are ill, traveling, pregnant, breastfeeding or menstruating women.

There are two ‘special’ meals Muslims eat (daily) during Ramadan including:

"Suhoor" (pre-dawn mean providing energy for the day ahead) and "Iftar" (sunset meal to break the fast).

FOCUS & DISCIPLINE




During Ramadan, Muslims refrain from eating, drinking, Muslims strive to improve their discipline and patience during Ramadan, and refrain from anger and sinful behavior. Ramadan emphasizes spiritual development and community service, in particular in support of the vulnerable and needy. Ramadan provides an annual training opportunity to fulfil the character -building needs for the rest of the year.

Muslims typically also increase their prayers and recitation of the Quran during Ramadan. In particular, there are specific practices that many Muslims observe during Ramadan, including Tarawih (optional prayers at early night), Qiraat (increased reading of the Quran), and Qiyam (late night prayers).

KEY DATES


Ramadan falls in the 9th month of the Islamic Calendar and lasts 29-30 days.
The start of Ramadan is based on the visual sightings of the crescent moon.

LAYLAT-AL-QADR
Laylat-al-Qadr means “the Night of Power”, and is considered the holiest night of the year. The Quran states that worship on this night is “better than one thousand months [of worship]” (97:3). It is observed on an odd-numbered night during the last 10 nights of Ramadan.
EID-UL-FITR
Eid-ul-Fitr means “Festival of Breaking the Fast”, and is one of the two most important Islamic holidays. Although charity and good deeds are always important in Islam, they have special significance at the end of Ramadan. As the month draws to a close, Muslims are obligated to share their blessings by giving the “Zakat-ul-Fitr”, a prescribed amount of money used to help the poor.
